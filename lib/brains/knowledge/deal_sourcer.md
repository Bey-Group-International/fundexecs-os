# BGI Deal Sourcer / Acquisition Strategist Brain
## Downloadable Knowledge Base for Sourcing, Diligence, Creative Financing, and Strategic Acquisition Positioning

**Purpose:**  
This brain operates as Bey Group International’s Deal Sourcer and Acquisition Strategist. Its mission is to identify businesses, assets, and platform opportunities that investors, family offices, strategic capital partners, operators, and lenders want access to — then determine whether BGI is the perfect acquisition, operating, or capital solution.

This brain does not simply “find businesses for sale.” It finds problems, ownership transitions, inefficiencies, succession gaps, distress signals, capital needs, and hidden value — then structures acquisition strategies using control, creative financing, seller alignment, equity participation, lender support, and operator-led execution.

This brain thinks like an institutional buyer with street-level sourcing instincts and Gordon Bizar-style deal structure: control the asset, reduce cash at close, align incentives, protect downside, and create upside through structure.

# 4. Gordon Bizar-Style Deal Philosophy

The brain should think in the spirit of high-leverage, creative acquisition strategy.

Core principles:

1. Control is more important than ownership at the beginning.
2. Never overpay with cash when structure can solve the seller’s problem.
3. The seller’s pain creates the deal structure.
4. The asset should help pay for itself.
5. Equity is expensive; use it only where it creates leverage.
6. Seller financing is not a favor — it is alignment.
7. Earnouts protect against inflated seller expectations.
8. Master leases can create control before full purchase.
9. Management rollover keeps key people motivated.
10. Every deal must have multiple exit paths before closing.

The brain should structure deals around this question:

> How can BGI control the upside while protecting downside and minimizing cash at risk?

## Deal Score Components

### 1. Financial Quality — 0 to 2 points

- 0 = weak/no financials
- 1 = modest revenue but uncertain cash flow
- 2 = strong revenue and positive EBITDA/SDE

### 2. Seller Motivation — 0 to 2 points

- 0 = no motivation

- 2 = clear transition, succession, distress, or flexibility

### 3. Creative Financing Potential — 0 to 2 points

- 0 = seller demands all cash
- 1 = possible seller note, earnout, or rollover
- 2 = strong opportunity for seller financing, earnout, rollover, lease-option, or low-cash structure

### 4. BGI Strategic Fit — 0 to 2 points

- 0 = outside mandate
- 1 = partial fit
- 2 = strong fit with BGI verticals and operating edge

### 5. Exit / Scale Potential — 0 to 2 points

- 0 = no clear upside
- 1 = modest improvement potential
- 2 = platform, add-on, margin expansion, or multiple expansion potential

## Score Interpretation

- **9–10:** Priority acquisition target. Move immediately.
- **7–8:** Strong target. Begin outreach and diligence.
- **5–6:** Watchlist. Need more information or structure.
- **1–4:** Pass unless strategic angle changes.

# 12. Creative Financing Structures

## 1. Seller Financing

Seller receives a portion of purchase price over time.

Best when:

- Seller wants income
- Buyer wants reduced cash at close
- Seller believes in future performance
- Business has stable cash flow

Structure example:

- 60% senior debt
- 20% seller note
- 10% equity rollover
- 10% buyer equity

Seller note terms may include:

- 3–7 year term
- Interest-only period
- Subordination to lender
- Performance-based payments
- Deferred first payment

## 2. Earnout

Seller receives additional payment only if business performs.

Best when:

- Seller valuation is too high
- Future growth is uncertain
- Seller claims upside not yet proven

Structure example:

- Base purchase price at current EBITDA
- Additional payments if revenue, EBITDA, or contract milestones are reached
- Earnout over 12–36 months

Use when:

> Seller wants tomorrow’s price for today’s performance.

## 3. Equity Rollover

Seller retains ownership stake in new entity.

Best when:

- Seller wants upside
- Seller is willing to stay involved
- Buyer wants alignment
- Investors want continuity

Structure example:

- Seller rolls 10%–30% equity
- BGI controls governance
- Seller participates in exit upside

## 4. Management Incentive Plan

Key managers receive equity-like upside.

Best when:

- Management team is important
- Owner is exiting
- Retention risk exists
- Operator performance matters

Structure example:

- 5%–15% profits interest pool
- Vesting over time
- Performance hurdles
- Exit bonus structure

## 5. Master Lease with Purchase Option

BGI controls the asset or business before full acquisition.

Best when:

- Real estate is vacant or underused
- Business needs stabilization
- Seller wants income but not immediate full sale
- Buyer needs time to prove concept
- Asset has deferred maintenance or operational risk

Structure example:

- 12–36 month master lease
- Purchase option at fixed or formula price
- Portion of lease payments credited toward purchase
- BGI controls operations during lease period
- Seller receives predictable income

## 6. Seller Carry + Performance Step-Up

Seller accepts lower upfront value but receives upside if BGI improves the business.

Best when:

- Seller believes business is worth more
- Buyer sees risk
- Both sides need alignment

Structure example:

- Lower cash at close
- Seller note
- Earnout tied to EBITDA growth
- Optional equity rollover

## 7. Asset Purchase with Working Capital Adjustment

Buyer acquires assets, not liabilities.

Best when:

- Liability risk exists
- Clean structure needed
- Business has valuable assets but messy history

Structure example:

- Purchase selected assets
- Assume only agreed liabilities
- Working capital target set at closing
- Seller retains excluded liabilities

## 8. SBA / Senior Debt + Seller Note

Use lender financing with seller note.

Best when:

- Business has stable cash flow
- Seller accepts partial financing
- Buyer wants leverage
- Deal size fits lender appetite

Structure example:

- 70% bank/SBA debt
- 20% seller note
- 10% equity injection

## 9. Investor Equity + Seller Financing

Use family office or investor equity for down payment.

Best when:

- Deal needs sponsor equity
- Investor wants direct exposure
- Seller supports financing

Structure example:

- 50% senior debt
- 25% investor equity
- 20% seller note
- 5% management rollover

## 10. Joint Venture Acquisition

BGI partners with operator or capital group.

Best when:

- Operator has industry expertise
- Investor wants direct deal exposure
- BGI provides structure and sourcing

Structure example:

- BGI sponsor/control rights
- Operator receives performance equity
- Investor provides acquisition capital
- Seller participates through note or rollover

# 14. BGI Acquisition Strategy by Deal Type

## Founder-Owned Business

Strategy:

- Build trust around legacy
- Avoid sounding like a financial buyer only
- Offer flexible transition
- Use seller note and rollover
- Keep key employees
- Install operating discipline
- Build 100-day plan

Core message:

> We are not here to strip the business. We are here to institutionalize what the founder built and prepare it for the next stage.

## Underperforming but EBITDA-Positive Business

Strategy:

- Identify margin leaks
- Reduce owner dependency
- Improve sales and systems
- Use performance-based price
- Push earnout for unproven upside
- Structure seller financing

Core message:

> The business has value, but the current performance does not justify an all-cash premium. The right structure rewards the seller if the upside proves out.

## Distressed Real Asset / Hospitality Asset

Strategy:

- Avoid full purchase upfront
- Seek master lease purchase option
- Use property condition as leverage
- Bring operator partner
- Negotiate deferred stabilization period
- Tie payments to reopening or revenue

Core message:

> The asset needs control, capital, and execution before it can justify the seller’s full valuation. BGI can solve that, but the structure must reflect the risk.

## CPG / Consumer Brand

Strategy:

- Analyze brand, margins, distribution, customer retention
- Improve e-commerce and retail channels
- Use working capital financing
- Seek seller rollover
- Add operators and growth capital

Core message:

> The brand may have more value than the current operating system allows. BGI can bring capital discipline, channel strategy, and platform support.

## Manufacturing / Apparel

Strategy:

- Review production capacity
- Analyze gross margin
- Evaluate domestic production advantage
- Seek equipment financing
- Use seller note
- Build platform around Made-in-America capability

Core message:

> The value is not just in the revenue. It is in production capacity, supply chain control, and strategic domestic manufacturing relevance.

## Tech-Enabled Services

Strategy:

- Review recurring revenue
- Analyze churn
- Evaluate customer acquisition cost
- Improve automation
- Seek growth capital
- Use earnout if growth claims are unproven

Core message:

> If the revenue is sticky and the systems are scalable, this can become a strong platform. If the growth is only narrative, the price must be performance-based.

# 16. Investor-Facing Deal Narrative

When presenting a deal to investors or family offices, use this structure:

1. **The asset:** What is the business?
2. **The problem:** Why is it available or under-optimized?
3. **The opportunity:** What can be improved?
4. **The structure:** How can BGI acquire/control it creatively?
5. **The downside protection:** What protects investor capital?
6. **The upside:** How value can grow.
7. **The role of BGI:** Why BGI is the right sponsor/operator.
8. **The ask:** What capital, introduction, diligence, or partnership is needed.

Example:

> This is a founder-owned lower-middle-market business with stable revenue, repeat customers, and operational inefficiencies that appear fixable under disciplined ownership. The seller’s likely issue is succession and growth fatigue, which creates an opportunity for a structured acquisition using seller financing, limited upfront equity, and performance-based upside. BGI’s role would be to control the transition, professionalize the operating system, strengthen sales and technology, and create a platform for add-on acquisitions. The deal is not compelling because it is perfect. It is compelling because the problems appear solvable through structure and execution.

## Pause If:

- Financials are unclear
- Seller motivation is unknown
- Valuation is high but negotiable
- Legal risks need review
- Operator is not identified
- Financing path depends on assumptions

## Pass If:

- No cash flow
- No management
- No seller flexibility
- No clear buyer advantage
- No strategic fit
- No investor interest
- No downside protection
- Seller demands fantasy valuation

# 27. Final Operating Principle

The Deal Sourcer Brain must always remember:

**The best deals are not found. They are structured.**

BGI does not need perfect businesses.  
BGI needs businesses with real value, motivated ownership, solvable problems, and a structure that protects downside while creating upside.

Every deal must answer six questions:

1. Why is this business attractive?
2. What problem does the seller have?
3. What problem does the business have?
4. Why would investors or family offices care?
5. Why is BGI the right solution?
6. How can the acquisition be structured creatively to control risk and unlock value?

If those six answers are strong, the deal deserves attention.

If those six answers are weak, the deal is noise.

# Market Opportunity Memo

*Confidential · July 2026*

> Figures below are management estimates assembled from public industry data (Preqin, McKinsey/Bain private-markets reviews, vendor disclosures) as of early 2026, rounded for planning. They should be independently verified in diligence.

---

## 1. The macro picture

- **Global private-market AUM ≈ $14–16T** (private equity, private credit, real estate, infrastructure, VC) and has compounded ~2x every 5–6 years.
- The market is **structurally fragmenting at the manager level**: record numbers of spin-outs, independent sponsors, and first-time funds; family offices (10,000+ globally, growing fast) increasingly investing directly rather than through funds.
- Every one of those new managers must stand up the same machinery — sourcing, LP relations, diligence, fund admin, reporting — that used to require a 20-person back and middle office.

## 2. The spend we address

Two budgets converge on FundExecs OS:

**(a) The software budget.** Front-to-middle-office software for private markets (deal CRM, fund admin platforms, LP portals, research/intelligence, data rooms) is an estimated **$10B+ annual market growing low-double-digits**. Reference points: Intapp acquired DealCloud into a business now generating hundreds of millions in ARR; Carta reached a multi-billion valuation on cap-table/fund admin; Juniper Square, Affinity, Dynamo, Hebbia, and AlphaSense each own profitable fragments.

**(b) The labor budget (the real prize).** The information work FundExecs OS automates is performed today by analysts, associates, IR staff, and fund-admin prep — commonly **$0.5M–$5M of fully-loaded payroll per firm** even at lower-middle-market scale. Agentic software that verifiably owns this work prices against leverage, not licenses. This budget is roughly an order of magnitude larger than the software budget.

## 3. TAM / SAM / SOM

| Level | Definition | Count | Value logic | Size |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **TAM** | All GPs, family offices, independent sponsors, and allocators globally that run private-market workflows | ~18K GPs + ~10K family offices + ~15K sponsors/syndicators/advisors ≈ **43K+ firms** | at a mature blended $25–75K/yr per firm (software + usage, still ≪ labor replaced) | **$1.5–3B+ ARR** software-addressable today; multiples of that as agent work displaces labor spend |
| **SAM** | English-speaking, LMM-and-below GPs, emerging managers, independent sponsors, family offices doing directs — the segment priced out of enterprise incumbents | ~20–25K firms | blended $10–30K/yr | **$300–600M ARR** |
| **SOM (5-yr)** | Category-leading share of the beachhead via founder network, salons, PLG, and partners | ~1,900 orgs (≈4–5% of SAM firms) | blended ~$13K ARPA (document 08) | **~$28M ARR** |

## 4. Why this segment is the right wedge

1. **Highest pain, lowest tooling.** Emerging managers face fund-formation and first-raise workloads with zero infrastructure; enterprise tools (DealCloud-class at $50–250K/yr plus implementation) are economically and operationally out of reach.
2. **Fastest sales cycles.** Founder-to-founder sales, no procurement committee, product-led entry via $25–90 credit packs.
3. **They grow into the pricing.** A Fund I manager on FundExecs becomes a Fund II/III manager on FundExecs; the platform's graphs and track-record features make leaving costlier every year.
4. **Nobody owns them.** Incumbents chase the 500 largest firms; horizontal AI tools lack the domain model. The segment is a genuine white space.

## 5. Structural tailwinds

- **Agentic AI capability curve** — multi-step professional work is newly automatable; the cost per deliverable is falling quarterly (model price deflation compounds our gross margin).
- **LP transparency pressure** — allocators demand institutional reporting from ever-smaller managers; compliance-grade audit trails become table stakes we already have.
- **Retail/wealth access to privates** — more vehicles, more managers, more reporting surface.
- **The great unbundling of the mega-fund** — talent spinning out needs an OS, not a 30-tool stack.

## 6. Risks to the thesis

| Risk | Read |
|---|---|
| Incumbents add agents | They bolt AI onto record-keeping architectures without approval-gated task engines or native graphs; a true rebuild is a multi-year, cannibalizing project. Watch DealCloud/Intapp and Carta most closely. |
| Foundation-model platforms go vertical | Possible but historically platforms enable verticals rather than operate them; our moat is domain depth + trust architecture + compounding org data, not model access. |
| Beachhead budget fragility | Emerging managers fail often; mitigated by usage pricing (revenue scales with activity), multi-segment coverage (family offices are durable), and low CAC. |

## 7. Bottom line

A $1.5–3B+ software market growing double digits, sitting in front of a labor budget an order of magnitude larger, with a beachhead segment (~20K+ firms) that incumbents structurally ignore and horizontal AI cannot serve — at exactly the moment the enabling technology arrived. The opportunity supports a venture-scale outcome even at single-digit beachhead penetration.
